# Hip Recovery — Bodyweight PT Routine
*Post hip labral tear surgery — later-stage rehab / return-to-function*

---

## Glutes & Hip Stabilizers

### Single Leg Glute Bridge
Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Drive through one heel to lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from knee to shoulder. Squeeze the glute hard at the top and hold for 2–3 seconds before lowering. Keep the opposite leg either bent (easier) or extended straight out (harder). The pause is key — it forces the glute med to stabilize rather than just momentum through the movement.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 10–12 each side

### Clamshells
Lie on your side with hips stacked, knees bent at ~45°, feet together. Keeping your feet touching, rotate the top knee open toward the ceiling as far as you can without your pelvis rolling back. Lower slowly. Small movement, big burn. Targets glute med and external hip rotators — critical for labral support.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 15–20 each side

### Side-Lying Hip Abduction
Same starting position as clamshells but with your top leg straight. Lift the leg to about 45° keeping your toes pointed slightly down (not up), then lower with control. Don't let the pelvis rock.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 15 each side

## Quads & Glutes (Compound)

### Bodyweight Squat
Feet shoulder-width apart, toes slightly out. Sit back and down, keeping your chest up and knees tracking over your toes. Focus on a slow, controlled descent (3 seconds down). At the bottom, pause briefly and drive through your heels to stand. For extra challenge, pause at the bottom for 2–3 seconds.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 15

### Reverse Lunge
Stand tall, then step one foot *back*, lowering your back knee toward the floor. Front shin stays vertical. Drive through the front heel to return to standing. Stepping back (rather than forward) reduces anterior hip stress — better for labral recovery than a standard forward lunge.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 10–12 each side

### Bulgarian Split Squat
Stand a foot or two in front of a chair or bed. Place the top of one foot on the surface behind you. Lower your back knee toward the floor by bending the front leg, keeping your torso upright. Drive through the front heel to stand. Brutal without any weight — hits glutes, quads, and hamstrings hard.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 8–10 each side

## Hamstrings

### Single Leg Romanian Deadlift (No Weight)
Stand on one leg with a soft bend in the knee. Hinge at the hip, sending your torso forward and your free leg back simultaneously — think of your body as a seesaw. Lower until you feel a hamstring stretch, then drive the hip forward to return to standing. Keep your back flat throughout. The balance demand alone makes this challenging and directly trains hip hinge mechanics.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 8–10 each side

### Nordic Curl (Assisted)
Anchor your feet under a couch or bed. Kneel upright, then slowly lower your torso toward the floor, resisting with your hamstrings as long as possible. Use your hands to catch yourself and push back up. One of the most effective hamstring exercises there is — start slow, these are hard.
- **Sets/Reps:** 2–3 x 4–6

---

## Hip Flexors

### Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch
Get into a low lunge position with one knee on the floor. Tuck your tailbone under (posterior pelvic tilt) to neutralize your lower back — this is what actually creates the stretch in the psoas. Hold, breathe into it. Post-labral surgery the hip flexors tend to get tight and guarded, so this one matters.
- **Hold:** 45–60 seconds each side, 2–3 rounds

### Active Straight Leg Raise
Lie on your back, one knee bent and foot flat, the other leg straight. Slowly raise the straight leg to about 45°, hold for a second, then lower with control. Strengthens hip flexors through range without hip compression.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 12–15 each side

### Dead Bug
Lie on your back, arms pointing straight up toward the ceiling, knees bent at 90° lifted in the air (tabletop position). Slowly extend your right arm overhead and your left leg out simultaneously, keeping your lower back pressed firmly into the floor. Return and repeat on the other side. Trains hip flexor coordination and core stability together — key for re-establishing hip control patterns post-surgery.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 8–10 each side

### Standing Knee Drive
Stand near a wall for balance if needed. Slowly drive one knee up to hip height in a controlled march, hold for 1–2 seconds at the top, then lower. Actively loads the psoas rather than just stretching it.
- **Sets/Reps:** 3 x 12 each side

---

*Focus on control over speed. If anything causes sharp or pinching pain in the hip, skip it and flag for your PT.*
